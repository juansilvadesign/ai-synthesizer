## Abstract

### Background

Advancements in digital cameras and the advent of smartphones have magnified the importance of clinical photography in facial plastic surgery. Here, we aim to examine the effect of different camera types, focal lengths, and distances from subjects on facial distortion.

### Methods

Twelve subjects underwent a series of frontal photographs using a smartphone camera and a full-frame digital single-lens reflex camera. Photos were captured at six distances from the subject. Seven focal lengths were used at each distance for the full-frame camera. Measurements of facial landmarks were made for each photo, with those made at 60 inches using the full-frame camera considered the gold standard and used for comparison.

### Results

Distortion of facial features using the full-frame camera occurred when photos were captured 8 inches away using short focal lengths. A 12%–19% increase in vertical stretching of the midface occurred when using focal lengths of 24, 35, and 50 mm (_p_ < 0.05 for all). The same features were distorted when a smartphone camera was used at 8 inches (18% increase, _p_ < 0.01) and 12 inches (12% increase, _p_ < 0.03).

### Conclusions

Distortion of midfacial features using both smartphones and full-frame cameras occurs with short, ‘selfie’ distances between the camera and subject.

## INTRODUCTION

Photography is integral to the clinical documentation, planning, and research of aesthetic and reconstructive surgery of the face.[1](#lary30935-bib-0001), [2](#lary30935-bib-0002) In the field of facial plastic and reconstructive surgery (FPRS), photographs are increasingly used as outcome measures by surgeons[3](#lary30935-bib-0003)\-[5](#lary30935-bib-0005) and in decision-making by patients. The digital camera has revolutionized photography, allowing high-quality images to be captured frequently and efficiently.[6](#lary30935-bib-0006), [7](#lary30935-bib-0007) With this, photography standardization in the clinical setting has become increasingly important to define.

A basic understanding of digital single-lens reflex cameras (DSLRs) and photographic principles is necessary for the facial plastic surgeon to achieve consistent results. Full-frame cameras (FFCs) contain a digital image sensor the same size as traditional 35 mm film cameras, allowing for wider fields of view, improved low-light performance, and less noise than cropped-frame sensors. The distance between the image sensor and the optical center of the camera lens is termed the focal length and affects the size of the captured field of view. The shorter the focal length, the greater the angle of view attained and vice versa. Photographic distortion can result from a combination of focal length and camera-to-subject distance.

The ubiquitous camera phone has resulted in a near-universal “selfie” phenomenon that influences self-perception. A deeper understanding of photographic definitions and distortions has become requisite for facial plastic surgeons. It is important to quantitatively determine what effect various digital camera settings have on the photographs produced. Factors including lens focal length and distance of the subject from the camera may alter the final image, but to what degree? Previous studies have creatively described the “selfie-effect” by positing a mathematical model to describe distortion in the horizontal plane;[8](#lary30935-bib-0008) however, more in depth study is warranted.

In this study, we aimed to quantitatively analyze the distortion of various facial regions in photos from DSLR cameras and smartphones at various distances and focal lengths. We experimentally quantify the degree of distortion associated with distance and focal length, which is relevant to surgeons who rely on clinical photography.

## METHODS

### Subject Recruitment

Approval was obtained from the Massachusetts Eye and Ear Infirmary (MEEI) Institutional Review Board (IRB) approval was granted prior to the initiation of the study. Human subjects were recruited from the MEEI staff to serve as volunteers for photographs.

### Photography

Photographs of twelve human faces were taken using a front-facing smartphone camera (iPhone X, Apple Inc., Cupertino, CA, USA) at various distances from the subject (8, 12, 24, 36, 48, and 60 inches away). Each image was taken in portrait orientation without any additional camera phone effects. The rhinion was placed in the center of the frame for consistency. A FFC (Nikon D810, Nikon, Tokyo, Japan) using several different focal lengths (24, 35, 50, 70, 85, 120, and 200 mm) was used to capture photographs at the same distances as above. All the subjects held a ruler in a standard position aligning with the mentum to allow for measurement normalization.

### Measurement of Facial Proportions

Measurements were performed using Image J software (National Institutes of Health, Bethesda, MD, USA). The measurements made are depicted in Figure [1](#lary30935-fig-0001). Vertical measurements included trichion to glabella (upper), glabella to subnasale (middle), subnasale to menton (lower), subnasale to stomion (SS), stomion to menton (SM), and nasion to nasal tip (NN). Upper, middle, and lower thirds of the face were then calculated. Horizontal measurements included ala-ala width (AA), interpupillary (IP) distance, intercanthal (IC) distance, and outer canthal (OC) distance. Normalization of distances between photos was achieved using 1 cm measurements of the ruler in each photograph. The following proportions were then calculated: upper/middle, upper/lower, upper/(lower + middle), SS/SM, AA/NN, and OC/IC.

### Statistical Analysis

We sought to identify differences in facial proportions when focal length and distance from the subject varied. Measurements made at 60 inches away from the subject (the furthest distance tested) were deemed the ‘gold standard’, given distortion increases the closer the subject is to the lens. As no distortional changes were found beyond 12 inches away from the subject, 60 inches away was sufficient to ensure a lack of distortion. For each focal length at each distance, the facial landmark ratios were averaged for the 12 subjects. These ratios were then compared with the gold standard ratios obtained from 60 inches away, with focal length remaining constant between comparisons. As data were normally distributed, unpaired two-tailed student t-tests were utilized. Smartphone metrics were compared with values attained from FFC subject-to-camera distance and focal length of 60 inches and 24 mm, respectively. The focal length of 24 mm was chosen as the comparison as it was closest to the focal length of the smartphone (28 mm). Statistical analysis was performed using STATA 15.1 (STATACorp, College Station, TX, USA).

## RESULTS

Forty-one photographs were obtained for 12 subjects. The majority of subjects were female (9/12, 75%) and white (9/12, 75%). Three subjects were of Asian ethnicity. Statistically significant distortions occurred in the midface when FFCs were used 8 inches away from the subject, compared with the gold standard of 60 inches. Subjects' glabella-subnasale distance (i.e., middle) was vertically stretched compared with the trichion-glabella distance (i.e., upper) using focal lengths of 24, 35, and 50 mm. There was a 12%, 14%, and 19% increase in vertical distortion at these focal lengths, respectively (_p_ < 0.05 for each). Figure [2](#lary30935-fig-0002) summarizes these findings.

## DISCUSSION

Clinical photography has been utilized in the field of plastic and reconstructive surgery since at least the 19th century, when Janos Balassa published photographs in his book _New Operative Methods of Nose Reconstruction_.[9](#lary30935-bib-0009), [10](#lary30935-bib-0010) Continual technological advancements of digital cameras have made photography an indispensable part of surgeon documentation, preparation, and outcome measurement. The prevalence of camera phones in the general population makes it important to assess their performance. Herein, we demonstrate minimal distortion of facial features with digital FFCs at a variety of distances and focal lengths. We also describe settings that would predispose to distortion using smartphone cameras.

Facial ratios were largely consistent for grouped focal lengths at each distance compared with the gold standard of 60 inches. The exception was for vertical landmarks (e.g., trichion-glabella compared with glabella-subnasale) at very close distances (i.e., 8 inches away) and short focal lengths (i.e., 24, 35, or 50 mm) (see Fig. [2](#lary30935-fig-0002)). For such parameters, the subject's midface is stretched vertically in a greater proportion than the forehead (see Fig. [4](#lary30935-fig-0004)). Such midface distortion particularly affects nasal appearance and should be considered by rhinoplasty surgeons during preoperative counseling. Attainment of FFC photographs _at least 24 inches away_ from the subject will avoid middle third distortion. The lack of distortion otherwise is reassuring for providers should any inconsistency be present in photo capture.

The percentage of Americans that own smartphones has increased from 35% to 85% in the past decade, according to the Pew Research Center.[11](#lary30935-bib-0011) By extension, attaining ‘selfies’ is now extremely commonplace given the prevalence of such smartphones[12](#lary30935-bib-0012); Boursier _et al_. report that 97% of Italian adolescents have taken a selfie, whereas McLean _et al_. found Australian teenage girls took selfies about once a week.[13](#lary30935-bib-0013) Google statistics reported 93 million selfies were obtained in 2014 alone, and this accounted only for Android phones.[14](#lary30935-bib-0014) Perhaps unsurprisingly, the annual AAFPRS member survey reported 75% of surgeons encountered patients seeking cosmetic improvements based on selfies.[15](#lary30935-bib-0015) Given these numbers, it is vitally important for the facial plastic surgeon to understand scenarios in which selfies can cause facial distortion. Ward et al. recently reported a 30% increase in the ratio of nasal breadth to bizygomatic breadth in selfies captured 12 inches away from the subject.[8](#lary30935-bib-0008) Herein, we further describe significant vertical distortion in patients' midface compared with the forehead when selfies are taken 8 and 12 inches away, which are natural distances for this type of photo capture (see Fig. [5](#lary30935-fig-0005)). Understanding these differences will allow surgeons to counsel their patients more thoroughly.

Facial plastic surgeons should take the above findings into consideration when designing their medical portrait studio. Appropriate photo capture for a clinical practice relies on a fundamental amount of knowledge regarding cameras, and subsequent consistency of as many variables as possible (choice of photographer, equipment, patient setup, preparation, etc).[16](#lary30935-bib-0016) Here we demonstrate that distortion can be minimized in the clinical setting when appropriate focal lengths and camera-to-subject distances are employed.

The results of this study must be viewed within the context of its design. A relatively small sample size was present, and as such, effect sizes had to be large in order for statistical significance to be achieved. Furthermore, the study cohort consisted of only white and Asian individuals. Although similar degrees of distortion would likely be present in other ethnicities, future studies using a broader subject group should be performed for confirmation. Additionally, iPhone X and Nikon D810 were the smartphones and FFC, respectively, used in this study. Consideration of additional or alternative types of distortion with other smartphones and FFCs must be made.

## CONCLUSION

This study examines the effects of distance and focal length on distortion of facial features using smartphones and FFCs. Facial landmark distortion with the use of FFCs was limited only to close distances when in combination with short focal lengths. In comparison, the use of smartphone cameras resulted in significant distortion of vertical facial features of the middle third when photos were captured at natural arm length for selfies. Facial plastic surgeons should be aware of such findings given the increasing prevalence of selfies in the general population.
