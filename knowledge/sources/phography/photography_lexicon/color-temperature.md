# Color Temperature & White Balance Explained

> Source: [Color Temperature & White Balance Explained - Hyph Tech](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=b1p95Cjyri4)

This guide outlines how to manually configure **White Balance (WB)** and **Color Temperature** in your camera to achieve accurate, professional-looking lighting for both video and photography.

### Core Concepts

- **White Balance (WB):** A camera setting that compensates for the color temperature of your light source to ensure "true white" objects appear white in your image (0:42).
- **Kelvin (K) Scale:** The unit used to measure color temperature.
    - **Lower Kelvin (e.g., 3200K):** Produces a warmer, orange-toned light (1:00, 1:18).
    - **Higher Kelvin (e.g., 5600K and above):** Produces a cooler, blue-toned light (1:02, 1:21).
- **True White:** Achieved when the camera's white balance setting matches the color temperature of the ambient light in the scene (1:32).

### Manual Configuration Steps

To ensure consistent and natural colors, avoid using "Auto White Balance" when you have control over your lighting (0:15):

1. **Identify the Light Source:** Determine the temperature of your lights (e.g., standard daylight is 5600K).
2. **Adjust Camera Settings:** Navigate to your camera's WB setting and set it to match the Kelvin value of your lights (1:27).
3. **Test:** Verify by checking white elements in your frame (e.g., white text on a shirt) to ensure they look neutral (1:45).

### Creative Lighting Techniques

Once you understand the basics, you can manipulate white balance to create specific "moods" (3:05):

- **Cool/Moonlight Effect:** Keep your camera set to 5600K while setting your lights to a high Kelvin value (e.g., 9,000K–10,000K), which adds a blue, nighttime ambiance (3:37, 4:03).
- **Warm/Cozy Effect:** Use lower Kelvin values on your lights (e.g., 4,000K) while maintaining a balanced camera setting to lean into a warmer, domestic look (3:56).
- **In-Camera Adjustment:** If your lights are fixed, you can manually offset the white balance to shift the entire scene warmer or cooler for artistic purposes (2:43).
